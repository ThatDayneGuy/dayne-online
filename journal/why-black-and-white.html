Why black & white

22 January 2026 — 5 min read

People assume black and white is a nostalgia setting. A filter you reach for when you want a picture to feel older or more serious than it is. For me it's the opposite: monochrome is the most present tense a photograph can be, because it removes the one thing that does most of the lying — colour.

Colour is loud. A red jacket will win every argument with a perfect gesture. A blue sky will convince you a mediocre composition is a good one. Strip the colour out and a photograph has nowhere to hide; it lives or dies on light, shape and timing. That honesty is uncomfortable, which is exactly why it's useful.

Colour shows you what things looked like. Black and white shows you what you were looking at.

The discipline of less

Working in monochrome changes how I see before I ever raise the camera. I stop scanning for colour and start scanning for tone — where the light falls, where it doesn't, the grey distance between the two. The world flattens into geometry and weight. It is, honestly, a more restful way to walk around.

None of this is a rule. Some pictures are about colour, and forcing them into greyscale is its own kind of dishonesty — this site's design follows the same logic, monochrome by default with colour only when it earns its place. But as a default, black and white keeps me honest. It asks the only question worth asking of a photograph: if this scene were reduced to light and nothing else, would you still care?

Most of the time the answer is no, and I keep walking. Occasionally it's yes. Those are the ones that end up here.
