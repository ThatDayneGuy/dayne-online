On waiting

14 March 2026 — 4 min read

Most of photography is not photography. It is standing somewhere slightly too cold, slightly too long, while nothing happens. The shutter is an afterthought — a punctuation mark at the end of a very long sentence made of waiting.

I used to resent this. I'd arrive at a corner, decide the light was wrong, and leave. Then I started timing myself. The pictures I actually keep are almost never made in the first ten minutes. They arrive around minute forty, after the place has stopped noticing me and I have stopped noticing my feet.

The picture doesn't happen when you press the shutter. It happens when you decide to stay.

There's a practical version of this — wait for the cloud, wait for the figure to enter the frame, wait for the bus to clear. But the more honest version is internal. You wait until your expectations have worn off. The photograph you planned in your head is usually the enemy of the one the street is willing to give you.

What waiting teaches

It teaches you the rhythm of a place. Every street has a pulse — delivery vans, school runs, smoke breaks — and once you can feel it, you stop chasing moments and start meeting them. It also teaches you that boredom is not a problem to be solved. It's the toll you pay to get past the obvious picture.

So this is the note I keep returning to, taped inside whatever bag I'm carrying: arrive early, stay late, and don't trust anything you saw in the first ten minutes.
