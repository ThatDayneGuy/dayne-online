One camera, one lens

8 November 2025 — 3 min read

A year ago I made a deal with myself: one body, one fixed lens, twelve months. No zooms, no backups, no agonising in the doorway about what to bring. The bag got lighter. Unexpectedly, so did the pictures.

The first month was mostly frustration. Everything interesting seemed to happen at exactly the focal length I didn't have. But somewhere around month two, the lens stopped being a constraint and became a way of seeing. I knew, before lifting the camera, exactly what the frame would hold. The composition started happening in my legs — three steps forward, one to the left — instead of in a zoom ring.

Gear choices are really attention choices. Every option you remove returns a little of your attention to the scene.

What stayed, what went

What went: the low-grade anxiety of options, the second body I never used, the sense that the right photograph required the right equipment. What stayed: everything that actually matters. Light still behaves the same. People still move through doorways at the same speed. The work, it turns out, was never in the bag.

I'm not precious about it — the experiment ends whenever a project genuinely needs something else. But the lesson is permanent: simplify the tools until the only variable left is whether you were paying attention.
